---
title: "Some Thorns Have Roses"
desc: "Recovery gave me things I didn't ask for and I'm not ready to give them back."
date: 2026-03-08
---

There's a thing nobody warns you about with recovery: you might not want it to end.

Believe me, I've been sitting with this for days, turning it over in my hands like something I'm not sure I'm allowed to hold. You spend weeks preparing yourself for the hardest physical experience of your life, you endure it, and then somewhere between the catheter bag and the third box of tissues, something shifts. You stop counting down and start paying attention. One day you realize you're grieving a season that hasn't even ended yet.

Maybe that's ungrateful. I don't think so. I think it means I was actually present for this, and the things I found — the strange, quiet gifts hidden inside the worst of it — are worth writing down before the rawness fades and I forget what it felt like to be this open.

Some thorns have roses. You just have to be still long enough to see them.

---

## Music That Breaks You Open

There's a difference between being moved by music and being *destroyed* by it.

Normally I can listen to Kainé from the NieR soundtrack and feel something — a pull, an ache, something that lives in the space between the vocals and the silence around them. Emi Evans singing in a language that doesn't exist, mourning something you can't name. That track has always hit me.

Recovery stripped the floor out. With the hormonal bottom dropped and nothing between me and the world, Kainé didn't just hit me. It went through me like I wasn't even there. I cried for twenty minutes. Not the polite kind where you dab your eyes and collect yourself — the kind where your whole chest is involved and you don't try to stop it because stopping it isn't a concept anymore.

I didn't hate it. It felt *alive*. Having your emotional filters completely stripped away turns music into a physical experience. Every note has texture. Every silence has weight. I've never heard anything the way I heard it during those weeks. The NieR OST became something I don't have vocabulary for when I had no defenses left. The whole room was the song.

That kind of vulnerability isn't sustainable. The filters exist for a reason. But I will miss hearing music like that.

---

## The Permission to Do Nothing

Let me just say it: having a catheter bag was, in its own bizarre way, a kind of freedom.

Not the catheter itself. That part was exactly as unpleasant as you'd imagine. But the *implication* — that my body was in a state where nobody, including me, expected me to do anything — I'd never experienced that before.

Most people never get guilt-free rest. Not really. There's always something you should be doing, some invisible productivity ledger you're supposed to be balancing. Even on vacation there's an undercurrent of "I should be making the most of this." Recovery obliterated that entirely. I couldn't do anything, therefore I didn't have to do anything, therefore I could actually rest.

It says something pretty damning about how I normally live that it took major surgery to give myself permission to just… sit. Watch the light change in the room. Let an afternoon happen without producing anything. I don't want to need a medical event to access that. But I'm grateful I got to feel it at all.

---

## Slowing Down Together

Recovery isn't a solo experience, even when you're the one in the bed. The people around you slow down too — they have to. You can't rush someone who can't walk to the bathroom unassisted.

In that enforced slowness, something opens up. Conversations happen differently when nobody has anywhere to be. You notice things about the people you love that you miss when you're both running at full speed. The way someone's hand feels when they're just resting it on yours, not reaching for anything. The quality of silence between two people who are simply in the same room, not performing togetherness but just *being* together.

Some of those days were terrible. I don't want to romanticize it. But the connection that grew in the constraint was real.

---

## The Fear Underneath the Grief

What I'm actually afraid of, underneath all the sentimentality: I don't know who I'll be on the other side.

The person who cried to Kainé for twenty minutes might not exist in six months. The person who could sit in a quiet room and feel the whole weight of being alive might get her armor back, piece by piece, until the world is manageable again. Comfortable. Filtered.

I'm grieving a version of myself I've only just met, and I don't know if she'll survive the return to normal.

Hormones are emotional armor. I understand that now in a way I didn't before. They're protective, and I'll probably be grateful to have them back. But they're also limiting — they decide what gets through and what doesn't, and you don't get to choose. When the armor was gone, everything got through. That was devastating and beautiful at the same time, and I'm not ready to pretend those are contradictory.

---

## The Shift

Early days were survival. I won't sugarcoat that. Counting hours, counting doses, counting the distance to the bathroom in shuffled steps. The body is a problem to be managed and the goal is simply getting to tomorrow.

Somewhere — and I genuinely can't pinpoint when — the posture changed. I stopped trying to get through it and started trying to be in it. Not because I'm enlightened or because I read some mindfulness book, but because the hard thing had gone on long enough that I ran out of resistance. What was left underneath was something I didn't expect: attention.

When you stop fighting a hard season, you start noticing things. The way light looks different when you've been in the same room for a week. The taste of the first real food after days of clear liquids. The particular miracle of walking twenty minutes when a week ago you couldn't stand. These aren't consolation prizes. They're the actual texture of being alive, and I'd been moving too fast to feel them.

I'm not selling toxic positivity. A lot of it was awful. But the awful and the beautiful were happening at the same time, in the same body. Learning to hold both without resolving either one might be the most important thing I've ever practiced.

---

## What You Actually Take With You

The rawness won't last. I know that. The hormones will restabilize, the emotional filters will slide back into place, and music will become something I enjoy rather than something that unmakes me. That's probably fine.

But the *practice* — looking for what's good inside what's hard — that's transferable. You don't need surgical recovery to ask yourself: *where am I just enduring instead of actually living?* You don't need your defenses stripped away to choose, even briefly, to put them down.

Strength used to mean pushing through, to me. Getting to the other side. Sometimes it does. But in that hospital bed, in the quiet afternoons, in the moments when Kainé was playing and I was falling apart and I wasn't trying to hold myself together, I found something else: the willingness to be present for my own life when it hurts.

I don't know how much of that I'll keep.

---

## Some Thorns Have Roses

I went into recovery dreading it and I'm coming out of it grieving its end. A month ago I would have thought that was insane.

That's not hypocrisy though. That's presence. That's showing up for a hard thing instead of white-knuckling through it.

Hard and beautiful aren't opposites. They never were. They're the same thread, and you have to hold still to see it.

Some thorns have roses. I saw them. I'm probably going to forget what they looked like. But I saw them.
