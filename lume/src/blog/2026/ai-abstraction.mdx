Working with generative AI means that we often operate at a higher level of abstraction than we normally do. It's natural that this happens. You describe what you want instead of how to get there, and the machine fills in the gaps. I've been doing this professionally for a while now and I genuinely don't know if I like this new level of abstraction.

A lot of it is focused on getting things _done_ rather than focusing on the quality or craft in the process. Sure, I'm more numerously productive than I usually am. I ship more. I finish more. But I keep wondering about how much of the soul gets lost along the way.

<Conv name="Cadey" mood="coffee">
  "Numerously productive" is a hell of a phrase for "I made more stuff but felt less while doing it."
</Conv>

When I write, I try to make people _feel_ something. My goal as a writer is to make you have emotions when you read what I put out there. Under a lot of this new stuff involving generative AI, the hard things get sanded down. Everything becomes homogenous. Things converge toward the numerical average of what they should be. That is not good for making people feel things.

Sure, you can actually make people feel things when you use a flow like this. I've done that recently. But something is being traded away in the process, and I think it's the texture — the rough edges, the weird phrasing, the choices that a statistical model would never make because they're too specific, too _human_.

I'm going to keep talking to you as an equal. That's been one of the most effective parts of my style: I write like I'm sitting across from you, not lecturing down at you. Generative AI defaults to lecturing. It defaults to the authoritative explainer voice that sounds like every other authoritative explainer voice. Resisting that pull takes conscious effort now in a way it never did before.

<Conv name="Aoi" mood="wut">
  So the tools are making it _harder_ to sound like yourself?
</Conv>

<Conv name="Cadey" mood="coffee">
  Not harder exactly. More like... the path of least resistance leads to sounding like everyone else. You have to actively choose to be yourself now.
</Conv>

Here's the part that really keeps me up: I know people trying to break into this industry as juniors and I honestly don't know how to help them. We are an industry that has historically valued quality and craft, yet somebody can yolo something out with Cursor and get hired by Facebook for it. The signal for "this person knows what they're doing" is getting noisier every day.

It feels like this part of the industry is marked by a bunch of doublethink. Nuance and opinions that don't fit into tweets. Senior engineers say "AI is just a tool" while their companies lay off the juniors who would've learned to use that tool responsibly. Leadership says "we value craft" while setting deadlines that make craft impossible without leaning on the machine. Nobody's lying exactly, but nobody's telling the whole truth either.

Let me just say it plainly: yes, I think using these tools at this level of abstraction costs us something essential. Not because the tools are bad — they're genuinely useful and I use them every day. The cost is that the default output has no soul. It's correct. It's competent. It's _fine_. And "fine" is the enemy of everything I care about as a writer and as an engineer.

<Conv name="Numa" mood="neutral">
  "Fine" is the ceiling that gets installed when you stop paying attention to the floor.
</Conv>

What I do know is that I'm using these tools deliberately, to investigate where the bar actually is. I want to see what's possible at this level of abstraction. Maybe seeing what's possible requires expensive tools and uncomfortable honesty about what they can and can't do.

The thing I refuse to lose is the voice. The weird, specific, occasionally self-indulgent voice that makes my writing _mine_. If the cost of higher abstraction is sounding like everyone else, I'll take the lower abstraction and the extra hours. Every time.
